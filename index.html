General overview: This project is a survey visualization project, where we collect student data and organize it in a way that we can evaluate clusters and how different clusters inform the opinions of others viewing the charts. Motivation: WPI Students are extremely invested in various clubs, departments, and Greek life organizations. Many students are looking for resources to find new things that they might enjoy on campus. That is why we create a web of connections between club members on campus to allow for easy recognition of where similar clusters of students tend to congregate. Students would be able to see these graphs and determine what other organizations are popular based on their current involvements and interests. Additionally, we look to determine the best graph visualization methods for different applications, including force-directed graphs, circular link graphs, hierarchical/tree graphs, and bipartite graphs.

Importantly, we learned visualizations of campus involvement require a highly significant amount of data. Despite the fact we got 130 responses to our survey, there was still a high proportion of groups with 1 or 0 representation. This is probably because there are so many programs and clubs on campus that it is hard to track all of them. Curiously, there was also a distinct sampling bias on the amount of representation of different clubs and programs. The makeup of the size of clubs and programs on campus does not necessarily map our graphs closely. These representations might more accurately represent individuals who respond to surveys within each group. We were able to successfully answer our questions about surveys and student group interactions through our variety of effective visualizations on campus commitments. We additionally were able to receive feedback about the efficacy of our visualizations using an independent survey of people who did not fill out the form information. These responses suggest that the Tree Map was the easiest to understand with the Force-Directed Graph being hardest to understand. In the future, the improvements we could apply to our visualizations would mainly be visual changes of connections and nodes.
